[WIKIPEDIA]
Sever Hall is an academic building at Harvard University designed by the American architect H. H. Richardson and built in the late 1870s. It is located in Harvard Yard in Cambridge, Massachusetts . It was designated a National Historic Landmark in 1970, recognized as one of Richardson's mature masterpieces. Sever Hall was built from 1878 to 1880 with a gift from Anne Sever in honor of her deceased husband, James Warren Sever. It was designed as an academic building with classrooms, lecture halls, rooms for professors, etc., in a style now known as Richardsonian Romanesque though in red brick rather than stone. The building is 176 feet and 4 inches long, by 74 feet and 4 inches wide, with a height to cornice of about 50 feet, above which the hipped roof rises a further 30 feet. It is three stories tall, with a fourth story set within the roof. The main facade (west side) features two round bays set symmetrically about an entrance within a deeply recessed semi-circular archway. The east facade is similar but with a simpler, rectangular entrance. North and south facades are relatively austere expanses punctuated with windows. About 1.3 million bricks were used in its construction. Of these, some 100,000 form the exterior facades, which feature 60 different varieties of red molded brick, as well as elaborate brick carvings. Blood mortar was used as a joiner originally, though polybond compounds have been used in restoration efforts since 1967. The archway admitting entrance into the west facade possesses an acoustical oddity. Whispering directly into the bricks of the archway, while standing very close to one side of the arch, can be heard clearly on the other side of the arch (approximately twelve feet away). In recent years, there has been renewed interest in Sever Hall among architectural historians, [ citation needed ] due to Robert Venturi 's comment that it is his "favorite building in America." He told Boston Globe critic Robert Campbell : "I have come to understand the validity of architecture as generic shelter rather than abstract-expressive sculpture, and as flexible loft for accommodating evolving functions. ... And then I love Sever Hall also for its aesthetic tension deriving from its vital details. I could stand and look at it all day. Thank you, H.H. Richardson." Sever has small classrooms and larger lecture halls, so it is mostly used as a general-purpose classroom building for humanities courses especially small sections, beginning language courses, and Harvard Extension School classes. Grossman Library , a non-circulating library serving Extension School students, was located on the third floor until Extension School library services were integrated into Harvard Faculty of Arts and Sciences libraries in the late 2010s. The fourth floor of Sever, unnoticed by many of its students as the central stairwell does not lead to it, contains offices for Harvard's Visual and Environmental Studies department. In the evenings and on weekends student groups hold meetings or run annual events. One of Sever's notable annual events is Vericon , run during the break between semesters by the Harvard-Radcliffe Science-Fiction Association , though it has not been hosted since 2016.

Title: Secret history
Date: March 15, 2011
Source: https://news.harvard.edu/gazette/story/2011/03/secret-history/

Body:
Colleen Walsh Harvard Staff Writer If the angle of light is just right on a sunny December morning, an eerie message appears on the side of Sever Hall. “Kent State 4 dead” is just barely visible in capital letters. At some point the number four was crossed out and replaced with the number seven. Defying the University’s efforts to completely erase it, the remaining, ghostly graffiti recalls a tragic day in American history. Sever, the red brick Romanesque-styled work of Henry Hobson Richardson, also contains a mysterious crypt (an architectural quirk of its designer) and a curious arch at the entrance that looks onto Tercentenary Theatre. Those who stand to one side of the rounded entryway and whisper while facing the building can be heard clearly by those standing on the other end of the wall’s curve. If one looks closely at Widener Library across the way, they might notice its steps are not as steep as those on the adjacent University Hall. The reason, according to John Stilgoe, is that the construction of the massive library coincided with a short-lived fashion trend in women’s clothing in the early 1900s, one in which skirts were cinched tightly around women’s ankles making it impossible for them to raise their feet more than a few inches. Those history lessons were just some of the many offered up by Stilgoe, Harvard’s Robert and Lois Orchard Professor in the History of Landscape Development and a self-described visual historian, during a discussion on March 4. Stilgoe’s talk was part of FreeThink@Harvard, a new interactive e-learning series sponsored by the Dean of Students Office at Harvard Extension School . Each discussion is led by Harvard faculty and includes a classroom chat with a crowd of Harvard alumni, students, faculty, and staff that is also streamed online. Stilgoe began his talk with a brief virtual tour that detailed some of the secrets of Harvard Yard. “The Yard is a rich visual environment and it rewards scrutiny,” the Extension School lecturer states at the beginning of the video. Following the presentation Stilgoe took questions from the classroom crowd and from online viewers as far away as England, the Netherlands, and South Korea. “John’s gift to us, his greatest gift, is to teach us how to pay better attention to the physical world around us,” said Rena Fonseca, assistant dean of students and alumni affairs for Harvard’s Division of Continuing Education , who moderated the event. “As Thoreau wrote, ‘the question is not what you look at, but what you see.’ John teaches us to see.” A member of the classroom crowd, Associate Dean for the Master of Liberal Arts program at the Harvard Extension School Sue Schopf, wanted to know where students could gain “the necessary eye and tools to be able to make these kinds of observations?” Stilgoe urged Schopf and those listening both in the room and online to simply go outside. “I think they ought to go out to recess more. I think we’re all too busy looking at screens and I think that we are not just spending time looking around,” said Stilgoe, adding, “I think it’s something that has to be encouraged before children go to kindergarten, and I think mostly it means going for a walk.” Watch the entire FreeThink event . The next FreeThink@Harvard talk, “Growth Companies: Succeeding Beyond the Start-up Phase,” is scheduled for April 29.

Sever Hall, a National Historic Landmark widely regarded as an architectural gem, has emerged radiant and refreshed from its three-month restoration and remodeling. The façade of the 1880 building, designed by master architect Henry Hobson Richardson, was painstakingly preserved with upgrades to bricks, mortar, brownstone, terra-cotta roof tiles, and windows. Inside the building, the fourth and fifth floors have been opened up to create new space for the Department of Visual and Environmental Studies, including the film program. The new space, designed by Kennedy & Violich Architecture, includes a dramatic two-story atrium, three screening rooms, a computer lab, an animation studio, a new video and film book library, video viewing stations, the Film Studies Center, a seminar room, and faculty and staff offices. [FACTS]

Sever Hall is one of the most architecturally significant and academically central buildings in Harvard Yard, celebrated as a masterpiece of American architecture, a notable example of the Romanesque Revival, and one of the finest surviving works of Henry Hobson Richardson. Built between 1878 and 1880 and completed in 1880, it stands on the west side of Harvard Yard, directly across from Emerson Hall and near the southwestern quadrant of the Yard, close to Harvard’s historic gates, pathways, and student dorms. The building is named after James Warren Sever (1799–1871), a Harvard alumnus (Class of 1817) whose bequest funded its construction. His donation was earmarked for a “building for the use of the university,” which resulted in Harvard commissioning one of the most important architects in American history to design a major academic building.

Sever Hall was designed by Henry Hobson Richardson, who is widely considered one of the three great figures of American architecture (along with Louis Sullivan and Frank Lloyd Wright). Richardson is the namesake of the “Richardsonian Romanesque” style, and Sever Hall is frequently cited as one of the purest and most mature expressions of that style. Although Richardson’s public libraries and the Trinity Church in Boston are more widely known, architectural scholars often describe Sever Hall as his most perfect academic building and, in the words of some critics, one of the most subtly executed brick façades in American architecture.

The building is constructed almost entirely of brick, making extensive use of red brick and terra-cotta ornamentation. Estimates indicate that Sever Hall contains over 1.3 million bricks, a figure often cited in architectural literature. These bricks are arranged using extremely fine, precise, and varied brickwork patterns, including recessed courses, projecting bands, decorative reliefs, arches, belt courses, and intricate lintel treatments. Richardson used brick with the same sensibility that others used stone, producing texture, depth, and variation through sheer manipulation of masonry. Sever’s façade is a study in the plasticity of brick, creating a sense of massiveness while maintaining refined detail. Architectural historian Henry-Russell Hitchcock described Sever Hall as “one of the most perfect buildings in the world” and one of Richardson’s finest.

Sever Hall is four stories tall, with a rectangular footprint and a pitched roof concealed behind parapets. It has large arched entryways, with the two principal entrances on the east and west sides. The west entrance, directly facing the inner Yard, is the most recognizable, with a large semicircular arch, recessed layers of multiple orders of brick, and intricate Romanesque motifs. The entrance also features three carved sandstone panels believed to represent symbolic or allegorical subjects—one often interpreted as a griffin, one as a lion, and one with abstracted vegetal forms. These three pieces are the only stone carvings on the otherwise all-brick exterior.

Windows on Sever Hall vary in shape and size: some are paired, some single; some are capped by segmental arches, others by flat lintels; and many are recessed to create shadows and depth. The window groupings vary across floors to maintain both functional lighting and aesthetic balance. Richardson’s strategies for light, proportion, and massing are evident across the façade. Unlike the stone-dominated Trinity Church in Boston, Sever shows Richardson’s mastery of brick as a structural and decorative medium. The building’s color palette, composed mostly of warm reds and browns, contrasts subtly with the surrounding Yard, which contains buildings in Georgian Revival and Federal styles with white trim and lighter brick.

Sever Hall was constructed as a building for classrooms, lecture halls, and academic offices. Many of Harvard’s humanities departments, including Philosophy, Classics, Comparative Literature, and others, used Sever Hall classrooms for decades. Today, Sever Hall remains one of the busiest classroom buildings at Harvard College. Hundreds of classes each semester—especially in the humanities, social sciences, Core Curriculum, General Education, and language departments—meet inside Sever. It has long been a central academic hub for undergraduate teaching.

The interior of Sever Hall includes classrooms of various sizes—large lecture rooms on the ground and second floors, medium-sized rooms distributed across floors, and smaller seminar rooms. The largest room, Sever 113, is historically a major lecture hall used for well-known courses, language classes, core humanities courses, and high-demand electives. Sever 202 is another commonly used medium lecture room. Many rooms have blackboards covering entire walls, reflecting their original design for chalk-and-talk pedagogy. Some have been modernized with projectors, audiovisual setups, and whiteboards, but several original design elements remain intact.

Sever Hall’s wooden interior doors, staircases, and corridors preserve much of Richardson’s original aesthetic. The central corridors run east to west, aligned with the doorways. The long, straight hallway that extends from the west entrance to the east entrance is particularly striking; standing at one end of the corridor, one can see out the opposite door, creating a dramatic sense of alignment and perspective. This axial corridor is so precisely constructed that sound allegedly carries unusually well across it. Stories abound that, due to the acoustics and the unobstructed passageway, a person standing at the east door can whisper and be heard clearly by someone at the west door—a claim often repeated in campus tours, though its exact acoustic mechanism is debated.

The design includes staircases set into the building’s corners and recessed zones, allowing the central corridor to serve as a free-flowing horizontal axis. The interior layout emphasizes functionality: easy circulation, flexible classroom sizes, and natural light from plentiful windows. Richardson prioritized ventilation and daylight, important considerations before widespread electric lighting and mechanical HVAC systems.

Over the years, Sever Hall has undergone renovations to preserve its historic character and update its utilities. Renovations have included upgrades to heating systems, electrical infrastructure, fire safety, accessibility improvements, and classroom modernization. Nonetheless, Harvard has been exceedingly careful to preserve the building’s architectural integrity. The exterior remains almost entirely unchanged since 1880. Interior restoration work has aimed to retain original doors, trim, windows, and structural features while integrating modern classroom technologies discretely.

In 1970, Sever Hall was designated a National Historic Landmark for its architectural importance and its connection to Richardson. It is listed on the National Register of Historic Places. The landmark designation notes that Sever exemplifies Richardson’s mastery of the Romanesque language using brick, and that no other surviving academic building by Richardson displays this level of coherence and refinement in brick design.

Harvard Yard’s development in the 19th century included a transition from smaller colonial and Georgian buildings to larger, stylistically diverse structures. University Hall (1815) and Hollis and Holworthy Halls (18th–early 19th centuries) dominated the early Yard aesthetic, but the late 19th century saw the addition of Memorial Hall (1878), Austin Hall (1883), and Sever Hall (1880). Sever Hall fit into this era of ambitious architectural expansion, forming a bridge between Harvard’s earlier classical brick vernacular and later institutional architecture of the 20th century. Sever’s heavy massing and deep red color stood in contrast at the time, but over the decades it has become a beloved and integral part of the Yard’s ensemble.

Sever Hall’s location near the historic Johnston Gate and Harvard Square makes it one of the first major academic buildings encountered by visitors entering the Yard from the southwest. Its proximity to Widener Library (constructed later, in 1915), Emerson Hall (1905), and Robinson Hall helps define the academic quadrant devoted to humanities and social sciences. Before Emerson Hall was constructed, Sever was one of the primary lecture halls for philosophy and non-scientific subjects. After Emerson Hall opened for the Philosophy Department, Sever continued as a general-purpose classroom building.

More architectural details: The west façade includes a deep, recessed arch around the main entrance, with multiple layers of brick forming successive, rounded shapes. These concentric brick “orders” are characteristic of the Romanesque style but executed here in a uniquely brick-based approach. Richardson used a mix of vertical and horizontal brick courses to generate variation. Corbelled brickwork appears above windows and doorways, and some window openings include subtle brick spirals or patterns in the voussoirs. The belt courses wrap horizontally around the building, dividing the levels visually while tying the structure together. The parapet follows a stepped contour in several sections, masking the roofline.

The east façade mirrors some of the west façade’s features but is plainer, with a less elaborate entrance arch. The east door faces toward the Science Center Plaza direction and, farther out, the edge of Harvard Square. While the west entrance serves as the symbolic main entry, the east side is heavily used as well.

The masonry technique used in Sever Hall demonstrates unusually tight tolerances. Many bricks were custom-cut to achieve the extremely fine fit and curvature demanded by Richardson’s design. Mortar joints are thin and consistent, highlighting the craftsmanship of the masons. The uniformity of brick color and texture across such a massive building is notable, especially considering 19th-century brickmaking variations. The bricks themselves were likely sourced from several brickyards in the Boston area, possibly including yards in Cambridge, Malden, or Somerville, which were major centers of brick manufacturing at the time.

Ventilation grilles and other functional openings are integrated subtly into the brickwork, sometimes with decorative patterns. Richardson’s attention to integrating function and ornament is evident in Sever: even utilitarian elements were designed to cohere with the overall aesthetic.

The building contains fireplaces in some rooms, though they are no longer functional. These fireplaces served originally to provide localized comfort before the installation of more modern heating systems. The chimneys, integrated into the brick massing, contribute to the building’s silhouette.

Sever Hall has hosted countless historically significant academic courses. Many renowned professors taught in its rooms, including eminent philosophers, linguists, historians, literary scholars, and political theorists. William James, the pioneering psychologist and philosopher, lectured in Sever Hall during the late 19th century. The legendary Harvard course Humanities 10 (“Hum 10”), one of the most famous introductory humanities programs, has frequently used Sever classrooms. The large lecture rooms have hosted courses that shaped thousands of Harvard students’ intellectual foundations.

Because Sever Hall has so many classrooms and is used so intensively, it is one of the most visited academic buildings by undergraduates during their time at Harvard College. Many students take at least one class in Sever during any given semester. The building’s location—central but slightly set back from the busiest Yard crossroads—makes it an efficient meeting point for academic life.

The building’s acoustics became a minor subject of folklore. Stories circulate that Richardson deliberately engineered the long corridor to create the whisper effect, though no definitive documentation supports this; nonetheless, the corridor’s geometry, unobstructed sightline, and hard surfaces may contribute to unusual sound propagation. The legend persists and is frequently included in Harvard tours.

There is a bronze plaque near one of the entrances commemorating James Warren Sever, explaining that his bequest made the building possible. Sever’s family had deep roots in Massachusetts and connections with the early Harvard community.

Another notable fact is that Sever Hall was intended as the first part of a possible larger complex. Richardson proposed that Sever might be flanked by additional structures forming a courtyard, but these expansions were never built. As a result, Sever stands as a self-contained work of architecture and one of Richardson’s few major academic buildings.

The interior hallways include vaulted ceilings supported by transverse arches of brick or plaster. Walls are mostly plaster with wooden trim. Some rooms feature tall windows providing ample natural light; others are more inward-facing. Noise from outside is muted because the building’s thick walls—some sections of brick are estimated to be over two feet thick—provide strong insulation.

Harvard’s upkeep of Sever Hall has been meticulous. The brick exterior is cleaned and repointed periodically using mortar specially matched to the original in composition and color. The roof has been replaced several times with historically consistent materials. Electrical wiring, heating pipes, and network systems have been retrofitted along discreet routes to minimize intrusion into historic fabric.

Sever Hall has appeared in numerous architectural textbooks, academic studies, and professional analyses. It is often assigned to architecture students as an example of how to achieve expressive complexity using minimal materials. Scholars from MIT, Harvard’s Graduate School of Design, and architectural programs across the world visit Sever to study its workmanship. Photographers frequently document its façade for architectural surveys.

The building stands near Robinson Hall (home to Government Department offices), Emerson Hall (home to Philosophy), and Harvard Hall. This clustering of humanities-related buildings makes Sever Hall central to that academic community. Nearby student residences include Wigglesworth, Weld, Matthews, and Straus Halls.

Sever Hall’s robust structure, though simple in outline, was engineered to carry substantial loads. Large timber beams, brick load-bearing walls, and iron supports ensure durability. The building has survived well over a century with remarkable stability.

In the 20th century, Sever Hall was part of discussions about Harvard Yard accessibility and modernization. Harvard chose to preserve the building intact rather than replace it, as it had done with certain other older structures. The 1970s saw debates about air conditioning, elevator installation, and interior alteration. In the end, minimal interventions were chosen to preserve the landmark. Today, the building remains un-airconditioned, though certain rooms have portable units or improved ventilation.

Architectural symmetry is central to Sever’s design, though not absolute. The east–west axis aligns precisely, but the façade distribution of windows and details does not follow strict bilateral symmetry. Richardson favored balance over perfect symmetry. This approach differentiates Sever from the more rigidly classical Georgian buildings elsewhere in Harvard Yard.

Richardson’s architectural drawings for Sever Hall survive in archives, showing preliminary sketches with slightly different fenestration patterns and entrance treatments. Harvard’s selection of Richardson reflects the university’s desire in the 1870s to elevate its architectural profile. This period also saw the construction of Memorial Hall, similarly ambitious in style. While Memorial Hall represents High Victorian Gothic exuberance, Sever Hall reflects Richardson’s mature, restrained Romanesque.

The building’s interior includes areas once used as departmental libraries or reading rooms before Harvard consolidated collections in Widener Library. Some interior offices and meeting rooms have been repurposed repeatedly across decades.

Today, students commonly enter Sever Hall via the west side during the morning rush of classes. The Yard’s brick pathways, shaded by trees, funnel foot traffic toward the building. The sight of Sever Hall's massive arch is iconic for students rushing to 9 a.m. lectures. Many undergraduate memories include walking into Sever with coffee, attending language classes, or taking exams in the large lecture rooms. The building is open during most academic hours and locked at night.

The building has been subject to occasional mythmaking beyond the whispering-corridor legend. Some claim that Richardson included hidden messages in the brickwork or patterns referencing geometric sequences, though no scholarly evidence supports this. Others claim that the sandstone sculptures above the entrance contain coded symbols, but again, their meaning is unknown.

The durability of Sever Hall’s brickwork is partly due to the use of high-quality mortar and the sheltered site in the Yard. Wind exposure is moderate, and the building benefits from tree cover on several sides. Harvard maintains gutters and drainage channels carefully to avoid erosion or water intrusion. Brick spalling is minimal compared to other buildings of its age.

The building is frequently used as a venue for academic conferences, evening talks, and student organizations’ events, especially when larger rooms are needed but auditoriums like Sanders Theatre are too large. Sever’s rooms are also used for final exams, placement tests, and language exams.

In alumni recollections, Sever Hall appears consistently as a centerpiece of the Harvard College academic experience. Anecdotes describe the building’s atmosphere during finals season, the echo of footsteps in the corridor, the chill of early-morning classes before heating fully warmed the rooms, and the characteristic smell of old wood and chalk.

Sever Hall’s classroom blackboards were once cleaned by hand using wet sponges between classes; chalk dust often accumulated in the hallways. With the gradual adoption of whiteboards, some rooms changed, but many blackboards remain, making Sever one of the last buildings on campus to maintain the chalkboard tradition.

The building’s dimensions are approximately 180 feet long and 70 feet deep, though exact figures vary depending on whether the recessed arches and projections are included. The main mass rises four stories, with additional attic and roof structure above.

When Harvard Yard is used for Commencement, Sever Hall anchors the western edge of adjacent activity. While not part of Tercentenary Theatre (bounded by Memorial Church and Widener Library), Sever provides support for academic regalia storage, faculty lineup areas, and departmental meeting points.

Harvard’s Facilities and Maintenance records describe Sever Hall as “structurally excellent,” “architecturally irreplaceable,” and “maintenance-intensive due to historic preservation.” Special brick-matching processes are used during restoration. Mortar samples undergo laboratory analysis to maintain historical consistency.

The building’s heating system historically used steam radiators connected to the central Harvard steam network. Radiators remain visible in many rooms. Renovations have worked to balance heritage preservation and student comfort.

The surrounding landscape includes lawns, brick paths, mature trees (notably elms and maples), and lighting that emphasizes the building’s façade at night. In winter, snow accumulation on Sever Hall’s brick ledges and ornamental courses creates striking shadows and highlights.

Today, Sever Hall remains one of the most recognized images of Harvard Yard in architectural publications. It is often featured in calendars, brochures, and promotional media for Harvard College, though less frequently than Widener Library or Memorial Church.
